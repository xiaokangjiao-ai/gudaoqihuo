How AI Returns Prevention Is Quietly Reshaping E-Commerce Profitability

Published in Retail | AI Verticals Editorial

Amazon processed 1.3 billion returns in 2025. Each return costs the company an average of $21 in logistics, inspection, and restocking, and industry estimates suggest that 20-30% of those returns are preventable through better pre-purchase information, sizing accuracy, and product expectation management. AI returns prevention has emerged as the primary tool for addressing that preventable fraction, and the economic case for it is substantial: a 10% reduction in Amazons return rate would save approximately $2.7 billion annually. The technology works. The question is what happens to the customers who are the target of those interventions, and whether the efficiency gains are being distributed equitably between retailers and consumers.

The Economics of Retail Returns

Retail returns represent a $761 billion annual problem in the United States alone. Return rates vary dramatically by category: apparel returns run at 20-40%, electronics at 10-15%, and home goods at 8-12%. For every dollar of merchandise that is returned, retailers incur an average of $0.33 in processing costs. In the e-commerce context, where returns are three times higher than in physical retail due to the inability to physically inspect products before purchase, the cost of returns is one of the primary margins separating profitable businesses from unprofitable ones.

Traditional returns management focused on processing efficiency, how quickly returned items could be inspected, categorized, and returned to inventory. AI returns prevention represents a strategic shift: instead of managing returns efficiently, prevent them from happening in the first place. The shift is economically rational for retailers. It also creates a category of customer treatment that deserves scrutiny, because the interventions that reduce returns most effectively are often the interventions that create the most friction for legitimate customers.

Zalando Return Scoring and Customer Classification

Zalando, the European fashion platform, deployed a machine learning returns prediction model in 2023 that assigns each customer a return probability score at the time of purchase. The score factors in purchase history, product type, size ordering patterns, browsing behavior, and hundreds of other variables. Customers with high return probability scores receive different treatment: they may see targeted messaging encouraging them to review sizing guides, have free shipping thresholds applied differently, or have their orders flagged for additional quality control inspection before shipping.

The business results were substantial: Zalando reported a 19% reduction in return rates in the 18 months following deployment. The financial modeling suggested the reduction represented approximately 340 million euros in annual cost savings. But an investigation by the European Consumer Organisation in 2025 found that the return prediction system systematically flagged customers who had legitimately high return rates, not because they were gaming the system but because they were buying products with inherently high uncertainty: fashion for special occasions, items where fit varies significantly by brand, and customers in rural areas who could not easily access physical stores to verify fit.

These customers, disproportionately plus-size shoppers who face the greatest sizing uncertainty in online fashion, were receiving more friction in their shopping experience than customers buying standard-size items from the same product categories. BEUC filed a complaint alleging that the return scoring system constituted discriminatory treatment under the EU Digital Services Act. The complaint is pending. In the meantime, Zalando has modified its system to exclude body-size-related variables from the model, an imperfect fix since size ordering patterns remain a strong predictor of return probability even without explicit body measurements.

Loop Returns and the Return Abuse Detection Problem

Loop Returns, a returns management platform used by over 4,000 DTC brands, has introduced AI-powered return reason analysis that goes beyond categorizing returns to predicting future return behavior and recommending interventions at the individual customer level. Loop system can identify that a customer who has returned three orders from a particular brand in the past 90 days has an 87% probability of returning the current order, and can trigger an automated intervention from a size exchange offer to a proactive customer service outreach.

The interventions work: Loop customer data shows that proactive outreach reduces return rates by 31% among targeted customers. But the same data that enables targeted intervention also enables targeted abandonment. Loop return abuse detection flags customers whose return patterns suggest they are using e-commerce purchases as a rental service, buying items for a specific occasion and returning them. Once flagged, these customers may have return privileges restricted, be required to pay return shipping, or have their orders subjected to review that delays processing.

The definition of return abuse is calibrated by the vendor against its merchant clients interests, not against any external standard of consumer fairness. A customer who returns 60% of their purchases from a particular category is, by any reasonable business metric, an unprofitable customer. They are also potentially a customer with legitimate reasons for high returns, buying multiple sizes to compare, purchasing items for occasions that are then cancelled, or living in circumstances where the product delivered does not match the digital representation. The AI system cannot reliably distinguish between gaming behavior and legitimate high-uncertainty purchasing, and the business incentives point toward treating all high-return customers as potentially abusive.

What Prevention Costs Consumers

The aggregate consumer welfare effects of AI returns prevention are genuinely unclear. Retailers benefit from reduced costs. Consumers who receive frictionless service benefit from fewer intervention attempts. But a specific class of consumer, those whose legitimate purchasing patterns overlap with the behavioral signals that the AI associates with high returns, receives worse service, more friction, and potentially higher prices as retailers recoup the costs of return abuse prevention programs from their entire customer base.

The most defensible approach to AI returns prevention treats it as a cost reduction tool rather than a customer classification tool. Interventions should address the specific behaviors that generate returns, sizing uncertainty, product expectation mismatch, delivery reliability, rather than penalizing customers whose patterns correlate with return behavior without addressing the underlying causes. Size guides, fit prediction tools, and proactive communication about product specifications are returns prevention strategies that improve customer experience while reducing return costs. Algorithmic customer profiling that results in differential service treatment is a different category of intervention, and its costs and benefits should be evaluated separately.
