How AI Returns Prevention Is Quietly Reshaping E-Commerce Profitability

Published in Retail | AI Verticals Editorial

Amazon processed 1.3 billion returns in 2025. Each return costs an average of $21 in logistics, inspection, and restocking. AI returns prevention saved Amazon an estimated $4.7 billion in return-related costs in 2025. The technology works. The question is what happens to the customers who are the target of those interventions.

The Economics of Retail Returns

Retail returns represent a $761 billion annual problem in the United States. Return rates vary dramatically by category: apparel returns run at 20-40%, electronics at 10-15%, and home goods at 8-12%. For every dollar of merchandise returned, retailers incur an average of $0.33 in processing costs. In e-commerce, where returns are three times higher than in physical retail, the cost of returns is one of the primary margins separating profitable businesses from unprofitable ones.

AI returns prevention represents a strategic shift: instead of managing returns efficiently, prevent them from happening in the first place. The shift is economically rational. It also creates a category of customer treatment that deserves scrutiny.

Zalando Return Scoring and Customer Classification

Zalando deployed a machine learning returns prediction model in 2023 that assigns each customer a return probability score at purchase. The score factors in purchase history, product type, size ordering patterns, and browsing behavior. Customers with high return probability scores receive different treatment: targeted messaging, modified shipping thresholds, or order flags for additional quality control inspection.

The business results were substantial: Zalando reported a 19% reduction in return rates in 18 months. But an investigation by the European Consumer Organisation in 2025 found that the return prediction system systematically flagged customers who had legitimately high return rates: buyers of fashion for special occasions, items where fit varies by brand, and customers in rural areas who could not easily access physical stores to verify fit. These customers, disproportionately plus-size shoppers, were receiving more friction than customers buying standard-size items.

Loop Returns and the Abuse Detection Problem

Loop Returns introduced AI-powered return reason analysis that goes beyond categorizing returns to predicting future behavior. Loop return abuse detection flags customers whose patterns suggest using e-commerce purchases as a rental service. Once flagged, these customers may have return privileges restricted, be required to pay return shipping, or have orders subjected to delayed processing.

The definition of return abuse is calibrated by the vendor against its merchant clients interests, not against any external standard of consumer fairness. A customer who returns 60% of their purchases from a particular category is an unprofitable customer by any reasonable business metric. They are also potentially a customer with legitimate reasons for high returns. The AI system cannot distinguish between gaming behavior and legitimate high-uncertainty purchasing.

What Prevention Costs Consumers

The most defensible approach to AI returns prevention treats it as a cost reduction tool rather than a customer classification tool. Interventions should address specific behaviors that generate returns, sizing uncertainty, product expectation mismatch, delivery reliability, rather than penalizing customers whose patterns correlate with return behavior without addressing underlying causes.

Size guides, fit prediction tools, and proactive communication about product specifications are returns prevention strategies that improve customer experience while reducing return costs. Algorithmic customer profiling that results in differential service treatment is a different category of intervention. The companies getting this right combine predictive analytics with proactive communication rather than punitive intervention.
