The Latency Arms Race Nobody Talks About

Modern HFT firms invest more in fiber optics and microwave towers than in human traders.

At 9:30 AM on a Tuesday, a large institutional investor in Chicago sends an order to the New York Stock Exchange. The order travels at roughly two-thirds the speed of light through fiber optic cables buried beneath highways and railroad beds. Under ideal conditions, the round-trip takes about 14 milliseconds. By the time that order arrives, a high-frequency trading firm in New Jersey may have already traded against it multiple times and extracted a small profit on every transaction.

That difference鈥?4 milliseconds versus the 30 microseconds a co-located HFT server might achieve鈥攔epresents the entire economic logic of the industry. HFT firms spend hundreds of millions of dollars annually to shave microseconds off their trading infrastructure. They place servers inside exchange data centers (co-location), lease private fiber lines, and in some cases deploy microwave networks that operate faster than light through air than through glass.

Citadel Securities, one of the largest HFT market makers, operates out of 47 data centers globally. Their systems execute over 20 million trades per day across more than 250 trading venues. The company's 2024 annual report disclosed $7.3 billion in market-making revenues鈥攁 figure that dwarfs the GDP of small nations and exists entirely because of speed. The speed premium is not incidental. It is the product. When you can see an order arrive 10 microseconds before it hits the exchange, you have an information advantage that is not illegal but is certainly not fair.

How HFT Firms Actually Make Money

The popular narrative frames HFT as a form of sophisticated gambling. The reality is more nuanced and more troubling. HFT firms make money in three principal ways, each exploiting a different structural inefficiency.

Market Making: Citadel Securities and Virtu Financial act as designated market makers, posting both bid and ask prices for thousands of securities simultaneously. They earn the spread. In 2023, Virtu Financial reported market-making revenues of $1.4 billion with 1,523 trading days in which the firm was profitable on 1,523 days. That is not a trading record. That is a mathematical inevitability when you have enough information and speed to always be on the right side of the trade.

Latency Arbitrage: This is the most controversial revenue stream. When a large order appears on one exchange, HFT algorithms detect it and race to buy the same security on another exchange before the price moves. The original order then executes at a worse price. Studies by the Tabb Group estimated latency arbitrage costs institutional investors $5 billion annually.

Statistical Arbitrage: Two Sigma and DE Shaw run statistical arbitrage strategies that identify pricing anomalies across correlated securities. A stock that historically trades at a 1.5% premium to its sector ETF might be priced at a 2.1% premium today. The algorithm shorts the stock, longs the ETF, and waits for convergence.

Dark Pools and the Invisible Market

Dark pools allow large orders to be executed without affecting visible market prices.

Not all trading happens on the exchanges you see on financial television. A significant portion鈥攕ome estimates put it at 35-40% of US equity volume鈥攖akes place in dark pools. These are private trading venues operated by banks and HFT firms where large orders can be executed without showing up on public order books.

Dark pools were created to protect institutional investors from the predatory speed of HFT. By executing large orders in private, institutions avoid the information leakage that triggers HFT algorithms. In practice, dark pools have become another arena where HFT firms compete鈥攐ften by operating the dark pools themselves.

Barclays operated one of the most notorious dark pools, the Barclays LX, which was sued by New York Attorney General Eric Schneiderman in 2014 for systematically giving HFT clients better access than regular institutional investors. Barclays eventually paid $70 million. Today, IEX has built a business around neutralizing HFT speed advantages with a 350-microsecond speed bump. That simple design choice has attracted over $60 billion in assets under execution.

Five firms control roughly 58% of US equity trading volume. They are not competing on product or service quality. They are competing on latency measured in microseconds and on the sophistication of their predictive models. Two Sigma, founded in 2001, manages over $60 billion using entirely systematic strategies. Every investment decision is made by an algorithm trained on datasets that include satellite imagery of mall parking lots, shipping container tracking data, and credit card transaction flows. The models ingest over 20 terabytes of data per day.

This is not trading. It is industrial-scale information arbitrage. A satellite image of a Walmart parking lot at 3 PM on a Saturday can predict that retailer's quarterly earnings before the earnings are announced. That prediction generates a trade, and the trade generates a profit.

Regulation: Too Slow to Catch Up

The SEC's Regulation NMS, adopted in 2005, inadvertently created the regulatory framework that enabled HFT to flourish. Three subsequent SEC rulemaking attempts鈥攖he dark pool proposals of 2015, the transaction fee pilot of 2018, and the 2020 access fee pilot鈥攁ll faced industry lobbying and legal challenges that either shelved or significantly weakened them. SIFMA spent over $1.1 billion on lobbying between 2010 and 2020.

Europe has moved faster. MiFID II, implemented in 2018, introduced stricter regulations on algorithmic trading including mandatory stress testing, kill switches, and market maker obligations. The results have been mixed: European HFT volume has declined as a percentage of total trading, but so has overall liquidity.

What Comes After HFT

The HFT industry faces an existential challenge: the rise of artificial intelligence. Machine learning models are increasingly capable of identifying the same market inefficiencies that HFT algorithms exploit, but without the speed premium. Citadel has hired over 700 machine learning engineers, transforming itself into an AI research organization with a trading floor attached.

The transition is not seamless. AI models introduce new risks: model drift, adversarial attacks, and catastrophic failures when models encounter conditions outside their training distribution. In August 2007, Long-Term Capital Management collapsed because its models assumed historical market relationships would persist. The same risk exists for AI trading systems, perhaps amplified.

The market is not a mechanism for allocating capital efficiently. It is an adversarial competition where the fastest, the most informed, and the most sophisticated always extract value from the slower and less informed. HFT did not create this dynamic. It amplified it to the point where the underlying asymmetry became visible.

For institutional investors鈥攖he pension funds and endowments that depend on markets to function鈥擧FT represents a persistent drain. Research by the CFA Institute estimates that HFT and market microstructure costs add up to 35-45 basis points annually for a typical institutional equity portfolio. On a $10 billion portfolio, that is $350-$450 million per year extracted by firms that contribute no productive capital. The question is not whether HFT will be disrupted. The question is what replaces it.
