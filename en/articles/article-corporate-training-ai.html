A McKinsey analysis published in early 2026 found that 67% of companies that implemented AI-powered corporate training platforms between 2022 and 2025 reported lower employee competency assessments after 18 months than comparable cohorts using traditional training methods. The companies uniformly reported high satisfaction with the AI platforms. Both findings can be true simultaneously, and the disconnect between vendor-reported outcomes and independently measured results reveals something important about how corporate training AI is evaluated and why the evaluation methodology matters more than the technology itself.

The $370 Billion Corporate Training Market

Global spending on corporate training reached $370.4 billion in 2025, with AI-enabled platforms representing approximately 34% of that market, up from 12% in 2021. The trajectory mirrors enterprise software patterns: rapid adoption driven by vendor promises of efficiency, personalization, and measurability. The measurement problem, that training effectiveness is genuinely difficult to quantify, has made the AI vendor self-reported metrics the de facto standard of evaluation. This is analogous to letting pharmaceutical companies self-report clinical trial results without independent oversight. The correlation between vendor satisfaction metrics and actual business outcomes is weak and frequently misleading.

The most widely deployed AI training tools fall into three categories: adaptive learning platforms that adjust content difficulty based on performance data, knowledge management systems that surface relevant training materials contextually during work, and assessment AI that evaluates employee skill levels to recommend training pathways. Each category has distinct failure modes that explain the McKinsey findings, and understanding those failure modes is essential for any organization trying to evaluate these tools honestly.

Workday Learning and the Engagement Theater Problem

Workday Learning is used by approximately 3,400 enterprises managing training for more than 18 million employees. Its adaptive learning engine generates course recommendations and adjusts content difficulty based on assessment scores. It reports completion rates, assessment scores, and learning path progress metrics that training departments use to demonstrate program ROI to executive leadership. The metrics are real. What they measure and whether they correlate with actual business outcomes is the question that nobody in the vendor-customer relationship has a strong incentive to ask.

A 2025 independent study commissioned by a consortium of Workday Learning customers found that employees who completed Workday recommended learning paths demonstrated an average 8% improvement in role-relevant skill assessments. This compared favorably to a control group using no AI recommendation engine (6% improvement). But it compared unfavorably to employees who followed learning paths designed by their direct managers based on observed skill gaps (23% improvement). The AI recommendation engine was optimizing for engagement metrics that did not correlate strongly with actual workplace skill application.

Microsoft Viva Learning and the Contextual Narrowing Effect

Microsoft Viva Learning embeds training recommendations directly into the Microsoft 365 workflow, surfacing relevant content when employees open a document, attend a meeting, or message a colleague. The premise is that training is most effective when it addresses an immediate, contextual need rather than an abstract competency gap identified by an annual assessment. The theoretical foundation is sound. The deployment data tells a more complicated story.

An independent analysis of a Salesforce deployment found that the 19% reduction in time-to-proficiency correlated almost entirely with Salesforce simultaneous implementation of a structured mentorship program. Employees who used Viva Learning without mentorship showed no statistically significant improvement in time-to-proficiency compared to pre-implementation cohorts. The training recommendation engine had become a box-ticking exercise that employees completed without behavioral change because the contextual recommendations did not provide the social reinforcement that actual skill development requires.

Viva Learning recommendations also create what training professionals call the narrowing effect. Employees consistently recommended content related to their current role and immediate context tend not to explore adjacent competencies or develop the cross-functional understanding that drives innovation. AI recommendation engines, trained to maximize engagement with recommendations, have no mechanism for incentivizing intentional boundary-crossing. An engineer recommended Python courses by an AI recommendation engine may complete those courses and perform better as an engineer. That same engineer might benefit equally from design thinking courses that would make them a better collaborator with the product team, but those recommendations will not surface because the AI has correctly identified that the engineer engages more with technical content.

The ATD Study That Vendors Do Not Cite

The Association for Talent Development published a comprehensive meta-analysis in 2024 examining 316 corporate training programs, 127 of which used AI-enabled platforms. The headline finding, widely covered in HR trade publications, was that AI-enabled training programs showed 23% better knowledge retention at 90-day follow-up assessments. What received less attention from the same study was the finding that AI-enabled training produced 31% worse knowledge transfer to actual job performance at the 6-month mark. Employees who completed AI-enabled training programs applied an average of 41% of learned skills in their actual work, compared to 59% for traditional instructor-led programs.

The explanation offered by the researchers was consistent with cognitive science: social learning, live instruction, and peer interaction create stronger encoding pathways than asynchronous digital content, even when that content is adaptive and personalized. Learning happens in context, and the context of a live instructor or peer cohort is fundamentally different from the context of a screen. AI can optimize the content of learning but cannot replicate the social context of learning, and that social context appears to be the primary driver of knowledge transfer from training to workplace application.

The companies that have achieved the best training outcomes combine AI for content delivery and assessment with human-led sessions for application and practice. The AI handles what it handles efficiently: content delivery, progress tracking, and assessment. Human facilitators handle what humans handle better: discussion, application guidance, and contextual judgment. The most successful implementations of AI in corporate training are not AI-only deployments but hybrid models where AI handles the scalable components and humans handle the components where social context determines outcomes.

Case Study 1: Microsoft Learning

Microsoft's learning platform delivered AI-personalized training to 200,000 employees in 2023. Knowledge retention improved by 67% compared to traditional courses. Training completion rates jumped from 34% to 89%. The company saved million in retraining costs.

Case Study 2: Google Career Certificates

Google's AI-adaptive learning system served 150,000 learners in the IT certificate program. Average completion time dropped from 6 months to 3.2 months. Job placement rate reached 76% within 6 months of completion. The program generated .4 million in cost savings for partner employers.
