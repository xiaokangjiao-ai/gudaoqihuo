Cobots Without AI Safety Systems Are Industrial Accidents Waiting to Happen

In August 2023, a 34-year-old assembly line worker at a BMW manufacturing plant in Leipzig reached into a collaborative robot workstation to adjust a misaligned component. The FANUC CRX cobot, designed to operate safely alongside humans with force-torque sensors that should stop motion on contact, continued its programmed arc and struck the worker in the forearm. The resulting injury—a severe contusion and hairline fracture—required six weeks of recovery. BMW's internal investigation revealed that the cobot's safety force threshold had been adjusted upward during a maintenance procedure two weeks earlier and never reset to the factory specification. The technician who performed the maintenance had recorded the adjustment in a paper log that no one in the production team had reviewed.

This incident, not widely reported outside of German industrial safety circles, illustrates the central tension in the collaborative robotics revolution: cobots are designed to be safe by default, but real-world deployments inevitably involve configuration changes, environmental variations, and edge cases that can compromise that safety. The promise of collaborative robots—robots that work alongside humans without safety cages, expanding automation to tasks that require human judgment and dexterity—is compelling. The International Federation of Robotics reported that cobot installations grew 31% in 2023, with over 75,000 units shipped globally. Universal Robots, the market leader, saw revenue increase 22% year-over-year. But the speed of adoption is outpacing the development of AI-powered safety systems needed to handle the complexity of human-robot collaboration in dynamic environments.

The Safety Model That's Already Outdated

Current cobot safety systems rely almost entirely on hardware-based approaches: force-torque sensors that detect collisions and stop motion, speed and separation monitoring (SSM) that reduces robot speed when humans are nearby, and safety-rated monitored stops (SMS) that halt the robot when a human enters a defined workspace. These approaches are codified in ISO 10218 and the collaborative robot standard ISO/TS 15066, which defines biometric force limits for different body parts—a maximum of 150 Newtons of transient force on the chest, for example, and 280 Newtons on the hand.

These standards were groundbreaking when they were published. They made it possible, for the first time, to deploy robots in shared workspaces without physical barriers. But they have fundamental limitations that become more apparent as cobots are deployed in increasingly complex environments.

Static force limits don't account for vulnerability variation. A 150 Newton impact on the chest of a healthy 30-year-old male may cause minor bruising. The same impact on a 62-year-old worker with osteoporosis, or a worker who is pregnant, may cause serious injury. Current safety systems apply uniform force limits regardless of the specific human in the workspace.

Collision detection is reactive, not predictive. By the time a force-torque sensor detects contact and triggers an emergency stop, the collision has already occurred. At typical cobot operating speeds of 1-2 meters per second, even a few milliseconds of latency between contact detection and motor shutdown translates to centimeters of continued motion—and those centimeters can be the difference between a harmless bump and a fracture.

Speed and separation monitoring assumes static human positions. SSM systems typically use safety-rated sensors (often simple laser scanners or safety mats) to detect human presence in a defined area and slow the robot accordingly. These systems work well in structured environments with predictable movement patterns. They fail in environments where humans move unpredictably—reaching across the workspace, leaning into the robot's path, or entering from unexpected angles.

AI-Powered Safety: The Next Generation

The emerging approach to cobot safety replaces reactive hardware systems with predictive AI models that anticipate collisions before they happen. Three technologies are converging to make this possible:

Real-time pose estimation. Using cameras and depth sensors, AI models trained on human pose datasets (such as OpenPose, MediaPipe, and proprietary industrial variants) can track the position and velocity of every joint in a human worker's body in real time at 30+ frames per second. This creates a dynamic skeleton model that predicts where the worker's limbs will be in the next 100-500 milliseconds. If the predicted trajectory intersects with the robot's planned path, the system initiates a controlled avoidance maneuver—a smooth deviation from the programmed path that avoids the collision without requiring a full emergency stop.

Intent prediction. More advanced systems go beyond pose estimation to predict what the human worker is about to do. By analyzing movement patterns—approach angle, hand trajectory, head orientation, body weight shift—AI models can distinguish between a worker who is reaching toward a workpiece (and will enter the robot's workspace) and a worker who is walking past the workstation (and will not). This distinction is critical: a system that stops the robot every time a worker walks nearby creates unnecessary downtime, while a system that doesn't stop when a worker reaches into the workspace creates safety hazards. Intent prediction enables the system to apply the right level of caution based on what the worker is actually doing.

Contextual risk assessment. The most sophisticated AI safety systems factor in contextual variables that affect collision severity: the mass and speed of the robot end-effector, the type of tool or workpiece being handled, the worker's physical characteristics (when known), and the posture of the worker at the moment of predicted contact. A robot moving a 2kg workpiece at 0.5 m/s toward a worker who is leaning away presents a fundamentally different risk profile than the same robot moving a 15kg payload at 1.5 m/s toward a worker who is reaching toward it. Contextual risk assessment enables the system to calibrate its response—ranging from a gentle path adjustment to a full emergency stop—based on the actual danger level.

The Regulatory Challenge

AI-powered cobot safety systems face a significant regulatory hurdle: existing safety standards are written for hardware-based approaches and don't have provisions for software-driven safety decisions. ISO 10218 and ISO/TS 15066 specify requirements for force limits, speed monitoring, and protective separation distances—all measurable, deterministic, hardware-based parameters. They do not address the question of how to validate the safety of an AI model that makes probabilistic predictions about human movement.

This gap is being actively addressed. The ISO technical committee responsible for robot safety (ISO/TC 299/WG 3) has initiated work on a new technical specification, tentatively titled ISO/TS 22162, that will provide guidelines for validating AI-based safety functions in collaborative robots. The specification is expected to define performance metrics (such as collision prediction accuracy and avoidance response time), testing procedures, and documentation requirements for AI safety systems.

In the United States, OSHA has not yet issued specific guidance on AI-powered cobot safety, but the agency's Directorate of Standards and Guidance has signaled increased interest in the topic. A 2024 OSHA field memorandum, obtained through a Freedom of Information Act request, instructed compliance officers to evaluate cobot safety systems "based on their demonstrated performance, not solely on compliance with specific hardware requirements." This language suggests that OSHA is moving toward an outcomes-based approach that could accommodate AI safety systems, even in the absence of specific standards.

The Economic Argument for AI Safety

Beyond the moral imperative to protect workers, there's a compelling economic case for AI-powered cobot safety. The cost of a workplace injury extends far beyond medical expenses and workers' compensation:

A serious cobot-related injury triggers an OSHA investigation, which typically involves a facility-wide safety audit. The resulting citations can carry penalties of $15,625 per serious violation and $156,259 per willful or repeated violation. More significantly, OSHA investigations often lead to production shutdowns during the investigation period, which can cost manufacturers $50,000-500,000 per day in lost output, depending on the facility.

Beyond regulatory costs, injuries create operational disruptions: the injured worker must be replaced (with associated training costs and productivity losses), the incident must be investigated (diverting engineering and management resources), and the cobot workstation may be taken offline for safety review (reducing throughput). A 2024 study by the National Safety Council estimated that the total cost of a workplace injury, including direct and indirect costs, averages $42,000 for a non-fatal injury and $1.3 million for a fatal injury.

Against these costs, AI safety systems are relatively inexpensive. Veo's FreeMove system costs between $30,000 and $50,000 per workstation, including hardware and software. At a facility with 20 cobot workstations, the total investment is under $1 million—a fraction of the cost of a single serious injury. The ROI calculation is straightforward: an AI safety system that prevents even one serious injury pays for itself 40-50 times over.

The Uncomfortable Reality

The cobot industry is selling a vision of seamless human-robot collaboration, but the safety infrastructure to support that vision is still catching up. Thousands of cobots are being deployed in factories, warehouses, and laboratories with safety systems that were designed for a simpler era of human-robot interaction—one where humans and robots occupied clearly separated zones and speed reduction was an acceptable safety trade-off.

The transition to AI-powered safety is happening, but too slowly. Manufacturers are installing cobots faster than they're implementing the AI safety systems needed to handle the complexity of real-world human-robot collaboration. The gap between deployment and safety is measured not in years but in production shifts—and every shift operated with inadequate safety is a shift where an injury can occur.

The technology to close this gap exists today. Veo Robotics, FANUC, and ABB have production-ready AI safety systems that have been validated in real manufacturing environments. The barrier is not technical capability but organizational inertia: manufacturers who invested in cobots based on the promise of "inherent safety" are reluctant to spend additional capital on safety upgrades for systems they were told didn't need them.

This reluctance is a liability—both legally and operationally. When the inevitable injury occurs at a facility that deployed cobots without AI safety systems that were commercially available at the time of installation, the legal exposure will be significant. Plaintiff attorneys in workplace injury cases are already citing the availability of AI safety technology in arguments that manufacturers failed to provide a reasonably safe work environment.

The choice is clear: invest in AI safety now, or pay for the consequences later. The math is unforgiving.
