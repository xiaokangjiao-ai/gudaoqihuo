The Number Nobody Wants to Talk About

Corporate training budgets are growing, but so is the skills gap. Something is not connecting.

The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2023 estimated that 44% of worker skills will be disrupted in the next five years. McKinsey's 2022 reskilling report found that by 2030, between 75 million and 375 million workers globally may need to switch occupational categories entirely. LinkedIn's Workforce Learning Report found that 87% of executives believe they have a significant skills gap in their organization鈥攂ut only 31% of L&D leaders believe their training programs are effectively addressing it.

These numbers contain a paradox. If 87% of executives know they have a skills gap, why have not they closed it? The answer is diagnostic failure. Most organizations do not have a precise, data-driven understanding of what skills their employees actually have versus what their future strategy requires. They rely on self-reported skills, which are demonstrably unreliable. A 2021 LinkedIn data analysis found that only 35% of the skills listed on LinkedIn profiles accurately predicted whether a person would be rated as high-performing. The remaining 65% were either inflated, irrelevant, or absent.

LinkedIn's Skills Graph: A Map of Every Competency on Earth

LinkedIn has assembled the largest structured database of professional skills in the world. As of 2024, LinkedIn's Skills Graph contains over 40,000 standardized skills mapped across 900+ industries and 13,000+ job titles. The graph captures the relationships between skills: which skills frequently appear together, which predict success in which roles, which are trending in demand, and which are being abandoned.

LinkedIn's Economic Graph team has published research showing that skills-based hiring produces 35% better retention rates and 20% higher performance ratings than traditional degree-based screening. A software engineer with no computer science degree but demonstrated proficiency in distributed systems, Kubernetes, and Rust is statistically more likely to be a high performer in a cloud infrastructure role than a CS graduate with generic programming skills.

Degreed and the Learning Record Store

Degreed, founded in 2012 by Brian Uzzi and David Blake, pioneered the Learning Record Store鈥攁 database that captures every learning event an employee experiences, regardless of where or how the learning occurred. Unlike traditional LMS platforms that track formal courses completed, an LRS captures microlearning videos watched, articles read, podcasts listened to, mentoring sessions attended, conference talks given, and skills demonstrated in project work. An employee who has watched 47 hours of data engineering courses, completed three Kaggle competitions, and volunteered for two data infrastructure projects has demonstrated a commitment to transitioning into a data engineering role that a resume alone would never reveal.

Why Most Reskilling Programs Fail

AT&T has spent over $1 billion on a reskilling initiative called Future Ready, targeting 100,000 employees. The results were mixed: the completion rate for internal training programs was only 37%, and many employees who completed training did not actually apply their new skills in their roles. Amazon's $1.2 billion Career Choice program has been criticized for paying for credentials without career pathway integration. Employees who earn certificates in nursing or IT but cannot find positions that use them experience income disruption without durable employment benefit.

The common failure mode is treating reskilling as a training problem rather than a career architecture problem. Training without clear pathways鈥攚ithout jobs at the end of the learning that actually use the new skills鈥攆ails because adults are pragmatic learners. They invest time in learning when they can see a clear connection between the learning and a meaningful career outcome.

The skills gap is not a training problem. It is an information problem. Every employer has a workforce that contains far more latent capability than they are aware of. The challenge is not creating new skills. It is surfacing existing skills, connecting them to unmet organizational needs, and creating pathways for those connections to develop into careers.

The skills gap will not be closed by any single intervention. It requires an ecosystem: credentialing systems that are trusted and transparent, learning platforms integrated into work rather than separate from it, hiring practices that are skills-based rather than credential-based, and organizational cultures that treat every employee's development as a strategic asset.
