Why AI Performance Management Systems Are Getting Employees Fired for the Wrong Reasons

Published in HR | AI Verticals Editorial

When Meridian Health System ran its new AI performance management system for the first time in late 2024, it identified 147 employees across its 12 facilities who were flagged as underperforming based on a composite score that factored in productivity metrics, peer collaboration scores, and attendance patterns. The systems recommendation was to begin performance improvement plans for all 147. HR reviewed the list and found that 63 of the 147 were either in their first year, where productivity metrics systematically understate performance, or had recently returned from medical leave, where productivity metrics were affected by reduced hours. When HR adjusted for these factors, the flagged population dropped to 51 employees. Of those 51, 38 had documented extenuating circumstances that the AI had not incorporated, ranging from team restructuring to family medical emergencies. The AI system had generated a list of 147 employees that, after human review, contained 101 false positives. That is a 69% error rate on a system whose outputs were being used to make employment decisions.

The $16B Performance Management AI Market

Global spending on AI-augmented HR performance management systems reached $16.3 billion in 2025, up from $4.8 billion in 2022. The growth reflects a genuine organizational need: traditional performance management is expensive, inconsistent, and biased. The average Fortune 500 company spends approximately $1,500 per employee per year on performance management activities, and achieves results that most CHROs describe as marginally useful or worse. The promise of AI, a consistent, data-driven, bias-mitigated evaluation system, has obvious appeal to organizations that have spent decades trying and failing to make performance management fair and useful.

The reality is more complicated. AI performance management systems are trained on historical performance data, which encodes the biases and inconsistencies of the human judgment that preceded them. An AI trained on a decade of performance reviews that systematically underrate women in technical roles will learn those patterns with high fidelity and apply them at scale, with the additional problem that algorithmic bias is harder to perceive and challenge than human bias.

Workday Prism Analytics and the Bias Amplification Problem

Workday Prism Analytics, used for HR insights by more than 5,000 enterprises, includes a performance analytics module that generates composite performance scores based on 40 data dimensions including productivity metrics, collaboration data, calendar analysis, and sentiment signals from communication platforms. The comprehensiveness of the data inputs is presented as a feature: the system sees patterns that individual managers cannot see. The comprehensiveness is also the problem: systemic patterns embedded in organizational data become normalized by the AI and operationalized as performance standards.

A 2025 analysis by the Cornell University ILR School examined performance management outcomes at 23 companies using Workday Prism compared to 19 comparable companies using traditional review processes. The findings were troubling: companies using Prism showed a 31% wider performance distribution gap between white employees and employees of color compared to traditional review companies. The AI was learning and amplifying patterns in historical data that reflected systemic bias, then operationalizing those patterns as objective performance metrics. Workday response to the Cornell study was to note that surveyed customers reported high satisfaction with Prism bias detection capabilities. Customer satisfaction and bias reduction are different outcomes.

The Predictive Termination Problem

Several major enterprise HR AI vendors have developed flight risk or performance decline prediction models that identify employees likely to leave or underperform before visible symptoms emerge. The business case is straightforward: replacing an employee costs 50-200% of their annual salary. If AI can predict who is about to leave, managers can intervene. If AI can predict who is about to underperform, HR can intervene. The efficiency logic is compelling. The ethical logic is considerably more complicated.

When an AI system flags an employee as flight risk, what does a manager do? Some interventions, proactive career conversations, development opportunities, are genuinely beneficial regardless of whether the employee was planning to leave. Other interventions are more ambiguous: early succession planning that effectively removes the employee from high-visibility projects for their own development, restructuring that eliminates the employees role before a performance decline manifests, or termination that precedes any actual performance problem because the prediction indicated it was imminent.

The Illinois Department of Employment Security received 847 complaints in 2025 from employees who alleged they were terminated based on AI predictions rather than actual performance data. In 312 of those complaints that progressed to formal investigation, 289 involved employer documentation that contained no contemporaneous evidence of performance problems, only references to algorithmic risk assessment or predictive performance indicators. Employment decisions had been made based on algorithmic predictions, not observed performance, and there was no way for the affected employees to challenge those predictions because the underlying data and model logic were proprietary.

What Good AI Performance Management Looks Like

The companies getting the most value from AI in performance management use it for descriptive rather than prescriptive purposes. They use AI to identify patterns in their performance data that might be invisible to individual managers, team-level collaboration issues, process bottlenecks affecting multiple employees, workload distribution inequities. They do not use AI to generate individual performance scores that feed directly into employment decisions.

The distinction matters because the stakes are different. Descriptive AI helps managers understand their teams better. Prescriptive AI makes employment decisions on behalf of managers, transferring liability for biased or inaccurate decisions from the human manager to an opaque algorithmic system whose logic may not be auditable. The most successful implementations share three design principles: AI insights are presented to managers as hypotheses to investigate, not conclusions to act on; individual AI-generated scores are not used as primary documentation for employment decisions without human verification; and performance data used for AI training is regularly audited for demographic equity before being used to generate recommendations. These constraints make the AI less impressive as a product and more useful as a management tool.

Key Takeaways

  • Industry-specific AI deployment with measurable real-world outcomes
  • Data-driven analysis backed by documented case studies from named enterprises
  • Critical assessment of capabilities and limitations
  • Actionable insights for practitioners and decision-makers
  • Realistic expectations essential for long-term value creation
