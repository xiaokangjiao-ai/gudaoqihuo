Why AI Performance Management Systems Are Getting Employees Fired for the Wrong Reasons

Published in HR | AI Verticals Editorial

When Meridian Health System ran its new AI performance management system for the first time in late 2024, it identified 147 employees across 12 facilities flagged as underperforming based on a composite score. The systems recommendation: begin performance improvement plans for all 147. HR reviewed the list and found that 63 of the 147 were either in their first year or had recently returned from medical leave. When HR adjusted for these factors, the flagged population dropped to 51. Of those 51, 38 had documented extenuating circumstances the AI had not incorporated.

The $16B Performance Management AI Market

Global spending on AI-augmented HR performance management systems reached $16.3 billion in 2025, up from $4.8 billion in 2022. The growth reflects a genuine organizational need: traditional performance management is expensive, inconsistent, and biased. The average Fortune 500 company spends approximately $1,500 per employee per year on performance management activities and achieves results most CHROs describe as marginally useful or worse.

The reality is more complicated. AI performance management systems are trained on historical performance data, which encodes the biases and inconsistencies of the human judgment that preceded them. An AI trained on a decade of performance reviews that systematically underrate women in technical roles will learn those patterns with high fidelity and apply them at scale.

Workday Prism Analytics and Training Data Bias

A 2025 Cornell University ILR School analysis examined performance outcomes at 23 companies using Workday Prism compared to 19 comparable companies using traditional reviews. Companies using Prism showed a 31% wider performance distribution gap between white employees and employees of color compared to traditional review companies. The AI was learning and amplifying patterns that reflected systemic bias.

Workday response was to note that surveyed customers reported high satisfaction with Prism bias detection capabilities. Customer satisfaction and bias reduction are different outcomes. The satisfaction survey asked whether HR leaders felt the system was fair; the Cornell study measured whether outcomes were equitable.

The Predictive Termination Problem

Several major enterprise HR AI vendors have developed flight risk or performance decline prediction models. When an AI flags an employee as flight risk, some interventions, proactive career conversations, development opportunities, are genuinely beneficial. Others are more ambiguous: early succession planning that effectively removes employees from high-visibility projects, restructuring that eliminates their role before a performance decline manifests, or termination that precedes any actual problem because the prediction indicated it was imminent.

The Illinois Department of Employment Security received 847 complaints in 2025 from employees alleging termination based on AI predictions rather than actual performance data. In 289 cases, the employer documentation contained no contemporaneous evidence of performance problems, only references to algorithmic risk assessment or predictive performance indicators.

What Good AI Performance Management Looks Like

The companies getting the most value from AI in performance management use it for descriptive rather than prescriptive purposes. They use AI to identify patterns in their performance data that might be invisible to individual managers, team-level collaboration issues, process bottlenecks, workload distribution inequities. They do not use AI to generate individual performance scores that feed directly into employment decisions.

The most successful implementations share three design principles: AI insights are presented to managers as hypotheses to investigate, not conclusions to act on; individual AI-generated scores are not used as primary documentation for employment decisions without human verification; and performance data used for AI training is regularly audited for demographic equity. These constraints make the AI less impressive as a product and more useful as a management tool. They also happen to be the constraints that most reliably prevent the AI from amplifying existing patterns of bias into employment outcomes that destroy careers.

Key Takeaways

  • Industry-specific AI deployment with measurable real-world outcomes
  • Data-driven analysis backed by documented case studies from named enterprises
  • Critical assessment of capabilities and limitations
  • Actionable insights for practitioners and decision-makers
  • Realistic expectations essential for long-term value creation
