The HR Data That Changed Everything

Modern people analytics platforms ingest data from HRIS, performance systems, communication tools, and engagement surveys to generate workforce intelligence.

In 2016, a technology company with 8,000 employees implemented a people analytics initiative. The initiative integrated data from the HRIS, the performance management platform, the learning management system, the internal communication tool, the IT ticketing system, and the badge-in/badge-out physical access system. Every employee had a behavioral profile generated from these data sources.

The first finding: the employees most likely to be promoted were not the ones who worked the most hours or had the highest performance ratings. They were the ones with the highest network centrality in internal communication graphs鈥攖he employees who communicated frequently with colleagues across departmental boundaries. The second finding: employees who took vacation time in chunks of fewer than three consecutive days had 37% higher voluntary turnover rates. The third finding: managers whose direct reports had below-median collaboration diversity scores managed teams that underperformed by an average of 12% in the following quarter.

Flight Risk: Predicting Who Will Quit

Employee turnover is one of the most researched applications of people analytics. The cost of replacing an employee ranges from 50% to 200% of annual salary depending on the role. For specialized technical roles, the replacement cost can reach 400-500% of salary when you account for recruiting costs, ramp-up time, and knowledge transfer. A company with 10,000 employees and a 15% annual turnover rate is spending $75 million to $200 million per year on turnover-related costs鈥攁 number most finance teams do not have visibility into.

Modern flight risk models use structured HR data (tenure, compensation changes, performance ratings, promotion history) and behavioral signals (email response times, calendar pattern changes, Slack activity levels, project engagement). The behavioral signals are particularly predictive because they capture the psychological experience of disengagement before the employee consciously decides to leave. Workday has developed a flight risk scoring model that identifies employees in the top 10% of flight risk score. In pilot deployments, companies reported a 15-25% reduction in voluntary turnover among high-risk employees who received proactive retention outreach.

What Employees Do Not Know About Being Watched

The line between people analytics and employee surveillance is defined by transparency, consent, and purpose.

When a company analyzes an employee's email communication patterns to generate a flight risk score, does that employee know? In most organizations, the answer is no. The Electronic Frontier Foundation has documented the growth of people analytics tools that capture keystrokes, screenshot activity, microphone audio, and web browsing behavior. During the COVID-19 pandemic, when remote work became widespread, the market for employee monitoring software grew 55% according to Gartner. Some of these tools generate productivity scores that correlate with demographic characteristics in ways that would constitute disparate impact discrimination if used in hiring.

The key distinction is between analytics used to understand and improve the work environment and surveillance used to monitor and penalize employee behavior. The first is a legitimate HR function: understanding team dynamics, identifying overworked employees before they burn out. The second is employee surveillance, which erodes trust, increases stress, and tends to produce the exact performance degradation it claims to prevent.

The Manager Who Was Flagged and Fired

A large financial services firm implemented a people analytics system that included a manager effectiveness score calculated from: direct report engagement survey results, team retention rates, team performance against targets, peer feedback from cross-functional collaborators, and promotion velocity of the manager's direct reports. In Q3 2022, the system flagged a senior vice president managing a team of 12 people. His manager effectiveness score was in the bottom 5th percentile across the firm. High-performing employees who joined his team were leaving at elevated rates within 12 months. He was placed on a performance improvement plan and subsequently left the firm 14 months later. His team had gone through four complete rotations of junior staff in three years.

The case illustrates both the promise and the risk. The data captured something that annual 360-degree reviews had missed or softened: a systematic pattern of managerial dysfunction that was causing measurable harm to employees and organizational performance. But the data also raises uncomfortable questions: Was the SVP given a fair opportunity to improve? Who had access to the underlying behavioral signals? And what accountability exists for the people analytics system itself if its methodology is flawed?

The Limits of Prediction in Human Systems

People analytics is fundamentally limited by the fact that human behavior is not a stable statistical process. A model trained on three years of employee data is learning patterns that existed in a specific organizational context, during a specific economic period, with specific management practices. When any of those contextual factors change, the model's predictions may degrade in unpredictable ways.

The most common failure mode is data collision: the model uses proxy variables that correlate with protected characteristics in ways that generate discriminatory outcomes. A flight risk model trained on historical turnover data will learn that employees who took frequent medical leave had higher turnover rates鈥攁nd may deprioritize those employees for retention investment without distinguishing between ordinary illness and disability-related leave. The organizations getting people analytics right have invested as heavily in governance and ethics as they have in technology. They have established data ethics committees, conduct regular bias audits, and are transparent with employees about what data is collected and how it is used.

People analytics is not a performance management tool. It is a systemic health monitoring tool. The goal is not to identify the employees who are performing well and those who are not. The goal is to understand the conditions under which people do their best work and to change those conditions when they fall short. The unit of analysis is the organization, not the individual.

The transformation of HR from an administrative function to a data-driven strategic function is one of the most significant organizational changes of the past decade. The best HR departments today are generating insights that would have been impossible to produce five years ago. The challenge is doing so in ways that respect the dignity and privacy of the people the data describes. That challenge is not primarily technical. It is cultural and ethical鈥攁nd it will define the field for the next decade.
