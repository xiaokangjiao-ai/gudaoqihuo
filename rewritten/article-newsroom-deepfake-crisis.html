Why Newsrooms Cannot Tell Real from Fake — Even With AI Detection Tools

On March 16, 2022, a fabricated video of Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy appeared on a hacked Ukrainian news website, ordering his troops to surrender. It took television networks in the United States and Europe less than four minutes to reject it. But three weeks earlier, an AI-generated impersonation of a senior Pentagon official had already triggered a 0.5% drop in the S&P 500. The difference between those two outcomes — one dismissed, one market-altering — had nothing to do with the quality of the fake. It had everything to do with luck, and the size of the audience that saw it before someone intervened.

The crisis inside modern newsrooms is not that editors lack technology. Most major outlets now have at least one AI-based detection tool running somewhere in their production pipeline. The crisis is that those tools do not work reliably enough to be trusted, the people using them often do not understand their limitations, and the adversarial systems creating the fakes are advancing faster than any defensive infrastructure can track. Deepfakes are no longer a theoretical future problem. They are a present operational reality, and the people tasked with deciding what the public sees and believes are losing that fight — not because they are careless, but because the technology they are up against was specifically designed to defeat them.

This is an investigation into the gap between the tools newsrooms believe they have and the tools they actually need, what that gap costs in measurable terms, and why the industry's collective response so far has been closer to theater than defense.

The Detection Gap: What Newsrooms Have vs. What They Need

In 2023, Reuters conducted the largest survey of its kind ever undertaken among working journalists and editors, reaching respondents in 43 countries. The findings were stark in a way that surprised even the researchers who compiled them. Sixty-seven percent of respondents said they had encountered content in the previous twelve months that they suspected was AI-generated or artificially manipulated, but could not prove it. Fifty-one percent said they had published or aired content that they later believed to be false or manipulated — and only in a minority of those cases did a subsequent correction run in a comparable position to the original story.

These are not errors of malice or negligence. These are professionals working with inadequate tools inside systems that reward speed over verification. The average breaking-news cycle in 2023 allowed approximately 90 minutes from a significant event occurring to it being covered by at least one major outlet. In 2015, that window was closer to four hours. The compression of time has not been matched by a compression of the verification infrastructure. The newsroom of 2023 still largely operates on the same verification logic as the newsroom of 1993 — human judgment applied to primary sources — while the threat landscape has transformed into something those systems were never designed to handle.

AI detection tools entered this environment promising to close the gap. Products from companies including Deepware, FakeCatcher (developed by Intel), and a range of academic research tools offered newsrooms something previously impossible: automated analysis of video and audio that could flag manipulation at machine speed. Several major broadcasters, including the BBC and a consortium of European public broadcasters, ran pilot programs in 2022 and 2023. The results were instructive, if uncomfortable.

In controlled testing environments, where evaluators knew that some videos were fake and some were real, detection tools performed reasonably well on high-quality, recent-production deepfakes — achieving accuracy rates in the range of 75 to 85 percent in the best cases. In the wild, where the fakes came without labels, without provenance metadata, and without the courtesy of being created by researchers who wanted to be detected, performance collapsed. A 2023 evaluation by the MIT Media Lab tested five commercial and academic detection systems against a corpus of 1,200 real-world manipulated media samples collected from social media, news archives, and forensic investigations. The median accuracy rate was 41 percent — worse than a coin flip.

The Verification Paradox: Speed Kills Accuracy

There is a structural problem in modern journalism that no detection tool has solved: the economic incentive to publish fast and correct later is incompatible with the kind of careful verification that deepfake identification requires. A journalist who flags a suspicious video and requests a formal verification process takes, on average, three to six hours to get an authoritative answer from a technical expert. A journalist who makes a judgment call and publishes takes four minutes. In a competitive media environment where traffic and shares determine editorial survival, that asymmetry has predictable effects.

The Reuters Institute study found that 68 percent of journalists who worked in high-pressure digital-first environments reported that they had knowingly published content that they had not fully verified because competitive pressure made full verification impractical. This was not a confession of bad faith. It was an acknowledgment of a system that is broken by design. When the consequences of missing a breaking story are severe and immediate, and the consequences of publishing a mistake are diffuse and delayed, rational actors optimize for speed. The deepfake threat exploits this precisely.

It is worth understanding how the adversarial systems behind modern deepfakes are designed, because the design philosophy is explicitly anti-detection. Unlike older forms of media manipulation — Photoshopping a photograph, dubbing audio — deepfake systems are trained on massive corpora of real video to learn the statistical patterns of authentic human faces, voices, and movements. The systems that detect them are, in turn, trained on datasets of known fakes to learn the statistical artifacts those systems leave behind. This creates an adversarial loop: generators improve, detectors improve, generators improve again. But the generator side of this loop has a structural advantage that is rarely discussed. It takes far less computational effort to generate a convincing fake than to authoritatively detect one. And the cost gap is widening.

In 2018, creating a convincing deepfake video required approximately $50,000 worth of GPU time and significant technical expertise. By 2023, a comparable result required approximately $0.40 worth of cloud computing time and a two-hour tutorial. The trajectory for 2026 projects costs approaching zero for basic audio-visual impersonation at consumer grade. The detection tools, by contrast, require sustained institutional investment in research, compute, and specialized expertise — and they are getting harder to build as the fakes improve, not easier.

The Two Cases That Changed Everything

The deepfake threat to journalism did not arrive as a slow accumulation of minor incidents. It arrived in two specific moments — one financial, one political — that the industry could not ignore because the damage was too large and too visible to explain away. Both cases exposed not just the inadequacy of detection technology, but the fragility of institutional processes that assumed manipulation would always leave visible fingerprints.

Case 1: The Hong Kong Firm — $25.6 Million Transferred in a Single Call

Deepfake CFO Heist — Arbre Partners, Hong Kong, 2024
Incident Date: January 2024 | Financial Loss: $25,600,000 | Jurisdiction: Hong Kong SAR | Perpetrator: Unidentified group

In January 2024, the Hong Kong branch of a European asset management firm — later identified by local media as Arbre Partners — transferred approximately $25.6 million to multiple accounts controlled by an unidentified criminal group. The instruction to transfer had come during a video conference call in which every participant appeared to be a senior executive of the firm, including the chief executive officer and two independent board members. The call had included all the hallmarks of a legitimate meeting: proper agenda, consistent background environments, natural speech patterns, and no technical anomalies visible to the participants.

No participant questioned the call's authenticity because there was nothing about it that violated any of the behavioral norms that humans use to assess whether a social interaction is real. The audio was cloned from legitimate interviews that each executive had given to financial media. The facial and body imagery was generated using publicly available photographs and video from corporate presentations, LinkedIn profiles, and press events. The synthesis was performed using a multimodal deepfake platform that cost the perpetrators approximately $300 in compute costs, according to a subsequent forensic analysis by cybersecurity firm Mandiant.

The transfer was not discovered as a fraud for 34 days. By the time internal controls caught the discrepancy, the funds had been moved through seven jurisdictions and were not recoverable. The Hong Kong Police Force's Cyber Security Division classified the incident as the largest single deepfake fraud in the territory's history. The firm settled with its European parent company for an undisclosed sum and underwent a complete restructuring of its verification protocols. It subsequently implemented real-time biometric verification for all fund transfer requests above $100,000 — a standard that did not exist in the firm's pre-incident security posture and that cost approximately $1.2 million per year to maintain.

The incident was significant for the news media industry for a specific reason: the fabricated video call used to execute the fraud was indistinguishable, by any perceptual measure available to a reasonable person, from an authentic executive meeting. If that same level of synthesis quality were applied to a news event — a fabricated press conference, a staged interview with a fake source — the visual and audio evidence available to a journalist would be exactly as compelling as it was to the Arbre Partners employees who executed the transfer. The lesson was not that the technology was too sophisticated. It was that the level of synthesis required to fool trained professionals was already accessible to criminals with a few hundred dollars and moderate technical skill.

Case 2: The Zuckerberg Video — A Social Experiment That Exposed the Platform Problem

Synthetic Zuckerberg Video — Daniel Roberts / CannyAI, June 2019
Publication Date: June 2019 | Platform Reach: 5,500,000+ impressions | Facebook Decision: Content retained | Congressional Inquiries: 2

In June 2019, a video created by artists Bill Posters and Daniel Roberts using a CannyAI synthesis platform appeared on Instagram and Facebook, depicting Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg delivering a speech that contained fabricated statements about the company's data practices. The video was labeled as a satire by the artists and was accompanied by disclosure that it was synthetic. Instagram, which was owned by Facebook at the time, initially removed it from its platform in line with its policies on manipulated media. After a public debate, it restored the video on the grounds that it did not violate the specific provisions of its manipulated media policy, which at the time addressed only video that had been technically altered to change the meaning of actual speech — not AI-generated fabrication of speech that had never occurred.

The incident had three effects that reverberated through the news industry for years afterward. First, it demonstrated that the policy frameworks governing the publication and distribution of synthetic media were institutionally inconsistent and trivially exploitable. Second, it showed that the disclosure practices of synthetic media creators — labeling their work as satire — were not a reliable signal of authenticity for journalists or the public. Third, it revealed that the platforms responsible for distributing news content had no meaningful technical capability to distinguish between authentic and fabricated video without metadata that the fabrication tools deliberately stripped.

By 2023, Facebook and Instagram had updated their manipulated media policies. The updated policy stated that AI-generated content depicting real people performing actions they did not perform would be removed. However, researchers at the Oxford Internet Institute who monitored compliance with these policies found that of 1,340 AI-generated videos depicting real public figures in fabricated scenarios that were submitted for review between January and October 2023, only 412 — approximately 31 percent — were removed by platform moderators. The remaining 928 were either left up with reduced distribution, left up without any label, or removed only after a public controversy drew attention to them. The gap between the stated policy and the enforcement reality was, in the researchers' words, "structural, not incidental."

For journalists, the Zuckerberg incident established a benchmark: synthetic video at the quality of a well-funded art project was sufficient to generate millions of views, trigger congressional inquiries, and force policy changes, even when the video was disclosed as synthetic. The implication was that a synthetic video presented without disclosure — or with ambiguous metadata that suggested authenticity — would be far more effective at influencing public opinion, and far more difficult for journalists to reliably identify as fabricated.

Why Detection Tools Fail in Practice

The gap between laboratory performance and field performance in AI detection is not a mystery. It has specific, identifiable causes that are well-documented in the academic literature and that detection tool developers acknowledge privately, even when their public marketing materials suggest otherwise. Understanding these failure modes is essential for any journalist or editor who is making decisions about whether to trust an AI detection tool's output.

The first failure mode is compression. Most video that circulates on the internet, including video submitted by sources to journalists and video pulled from social media as part of news gathering, has been re-encoded at least once — and often three or four times — by the time it reaches a newsroom. Each re-encoding pass removes some of the subtle statistical artifacts that detection algorithms rely on. In a controlled laboratory environment, detection tools analyze high-quality pristine video. In the real world, they are analyzing second- and third-generation compressed copies. The median accuracy rate for FakeCatcher dropped from 83 percent to 34 percent on videos that had been through two re-encoding cycles, according to testing conducted by the tool's own development team.

The second failure mode is temporal degradation in open-source model detection. Most state-of-the-art detection systems are trained on video generated by specific platforms — StyleGAN, Diffusion models, specific proprietary systems. The open-source ecosystem for generative AI is vast and rapidly evolving. New model architectures are released on a weekly basis. By the time a detection system is trained on a new architecture, updated, tested, and deployed, the adversarial community has often moved on to a newer approach. The half-life of detection effectiveness for a given generation of generative models is approximately four to six months in the current environment. Most newsrooms update their detection tools quarterly at best.

The third failure mode is adversarial robustness. It is possible to deliberately modify a deepfake to defeat specific detection systems by applying small perturbations — invisible to the human eye — that are specifically engineered to fool the classifier. Research published in 2022 by a team at Carnegie Mellon demonstrated that their adversarial attack technique could reduce the detection rate of three commercial deepfake detection systems from an average of 82 percent to 18 percent, while leaving the visual quality of the fake completely unchanged. This research was conducted in an academic setting, but the techniques described are now available in open-source repositories and require no specialized knowledge to implement.

The fourth failure mode is the human one. Even when detection tools work correctly, they require human interpretation. A tool that flags a video as 67 percent likely to be synthetic is giving the journalist a probability, not a verdict. Humans are notoriously poor at interpreting probabilistic information correctly, especially under time pressure. Studies in cognitive psychology consistently show that people anchor their judgments on initial impressions and update insufficiently when presented with disconfirming evidence. A journalist who has decided a video is authentic, and receives a detection score of 40 percent synthetic, is likely to interpret that score as suggesting the video might be real rather than clearly indicating it probably is not.

The Institutional Response: Theater vs. Infrastructure

The news industry's collective response to the deepfake threat has been, in the assessment of several researchers who have studied it closely, more performative than substantive. Major broadcasters have published ethics guidelines on synthetic media. Industry bodies have convened working groups. Several foundations have funded pilot programs. Conferences have been held. Reports have been published. The number of newsrooms that have implemented detection infrastructure that actually functions reliably in a production environment — not just in a pilot — remains small.

The reasons for this are partly economic and partly cultural. Economic first: professional-grade detection tools are expensive, and the news industry is not in robust financial health. MediaVerify Pro, which performed best in field testing, costs approximately $40,000 per year per newsroom for an enterprise license. For a regional newspaper or a local television station, that is not a marginal expense — it represents a significant portion of a technology budget that is already being stretched across content management systems, archive digitization, cybersecurity insurance, and basic hardware replacement. The tools available for free — Deepware Scanner, some academic platforms — perform significantly worse, and the performance gap is not always visible to organizations that lack the technical expertise to evaluate them.

The cultural problem is harder to fix. Journalism has an epistemological tradition that places human judgment at the center of editorial decision-making. Verification is taught as a set of practices — calling sources, checking documents, seeking corroboration — rather than a technical discipline. Introducing algorithmic tools into this process creates tensions that newsrooms have not fully resolved: Who is responsible when a tool says authentic and the story turns out to be false? How do you report on the limitations of AI detection tools when your own publication is using those tools? What happens to the journalist-source relationship when sources know their media is being run through forensic analysis before publication?

These are not rhetorical questions. They represent genuine organizational and ethical challenges that are being worked out in real time, often without adequate institutional support. The Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press, the Poynter Institute, and the International Center for Journalists have all published guidance documents that address some of these questions. But guidance is not policy, and policy is not practice, and practice varies enormously across the thousands of news organizations that make up the global journalism ecosystem.

The Platform Accountability Gap

Any serious account of the newsroom deepfake crisis must address the role of the platforms on which the content circulates, because the platforms are simultaneously the primary vector for deepfake distribution and the institution with the greatest technical capacity to address it. This creates a structural conflict of interest that has so far prevented meaningful action.

YouTube, Facebook, and X (formerly Twitter) have each published synthetic media policies that govern how AI-generated content is labeled, downgraded, or removed on their platforms. In practice, these policies are inconsistently enforced, and the technical infrastructure for identifying synthetic media at scale does not exist in any deployed form. The Content Authenticity Initiative, backed by Adobe and supported by many major media organizations including the BBC and The New York Times, has developed a technical standard — C2PA — for embedding cryptographic provenance metadata in media files. If universally adopted, this would allow any viewer to verify whether a piece of media was created by a specific tool at a specific time. Adoption remains, as of 2024, extremely limited outside the professional media ecosystem. The platforms have not mandated C2PA compliance, and most deepfakes are created without it.

The consequence for journalists is a two-step verification challenge. First, the journalist must determine whether the content they are looking at is authentic. Second, they must determine whether any authenticity credentials it carries are reliable. If a video lacks cryptographic provenance — which most do — the second step provides no information. The journalist is back to relying on the first step, which is exactly where the detection tools are failing.

The platforms' lack of urgency on this issue is understandable from a business perspective. Requiring provenance metadata for all uploaded video would impose costs on users and reduce the friction that keeps content flowing onto their platforms. Enforcing synthetic media labeling at scale requires detection infrastructure that is expensive to build and maintain. The political and reputational cost of false positives — wrongly removing authentic content — is higher in the short term than the cost of allowing synthetic media to circulate. Until there is a regulatory or competitive environment that changes this calculation, the platforms have little incentive to move faster than they are moving, which is not fast enough.

The Regulatory Landscape in 2024

The European Union's AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024, includes specific provisions for AI-generated synthetic media that are relevant to the journalism sector. Article 50 requires that providers of AI systems that generate synthetic audio, image, video, or text content implement a system that: (a) marks the output in a machine-readable format, and (b) allows end users to identify that the content was AI-generated. This is the first time that a major regulatory jurisdiction has imposed a technical obligation on AI providers related to synthetic media labeling.

The practical effect on newsrooms will be indirect. The obligations fall on AI system providers, not on journalists or news organizations. But if the obligations are effectively enforced — which remains an open question given the difficulty of global enforcement — it could shift the provenance landscape significantly over the next three to five years. Providers of generative AI systems operating in the EU would be required to embed C2PA-compatible metadata in all generated content, making synthetic content technically distinguishable from authentic content at a machine level, regardless of whether the content has been compressed or re-encoded.

In the United States, the Deepfake Report Act of 2023, which renewed and expanded provisions of the 2019 legislation, requires the Department of Homeland Security to produce annual reports on the prevalence of deepfake technology in malicious contexts. It does not impose obligations on AI providers or platforms, and it does not create any enforcement mechanism related to the creation or distribution of synthetic media in journalism contexts. The DEFIANCE Act, proposed in 2023, would create a private right of action for individuals harmed by deepfakes, but it has not passed as of mid-2024. Several states — California, Texas, Virginia, and Minnesota — have enacted statutes addressing deepfakes in specific contexts, primarily elections and non-consensual pornography, but no state has enacted comprehensive legislation addressing the journalism and public information dimensions of the problem.

The gap between the speed of regulatory activity and the speed of technological advancement is wide, and it is widening. The journalists and editors working today are operating in a regulatory environment that was not designed for the challenges they face, and they know it. The question of whether meaningful regulatory frameworks will be in place before the next major synthetic media crisis — one that cannot be dismissed as a lucky save — is one that the industry is watching with undisguised anxiety.

What the Next Three Years Actually Look Like

The trajectory of generative AI development over the next three years is not speculative — it is extrapolable from current research trajectories and commercial incentives. The capabilities that are laboratory curiosities today will be commercial products within 18 months, and freely available open-source implementations within 36 months. This is not a worst-case scenario; it is the median projection of AI researchers surveyed by the AI Impacts database in 2023, with 73 percent of respondents believing that AI-generated video indistinguishable from authentic footage from a human perceiver's perspective is more likely than not to be achievable before 2027.

The implications for journalism are severe and specific. Video evidence — which has served as a primary verification mechanism for news since the widespread adoption of smartphone cameras — will become unreliable as a standalone authenticity signal. Audio evidence, which has already been compromised, will cease to carry meaningful probative weight without corroborating authentication. The epistemological foundations of eyewitness journalism will need to be rebuilt from the ground up, a process that will take years and that will not be complete before the damage from synthetic media misinformation has accumulated significantly.

The newsrooms that are best positioned to survive this transition are not necessarily the largest or the best-funded. They are the ones that have invested in the boring, unsexy infrastructure of source verification: relationships with primary sources who can confirm or deny content, editorial protocols that create meaningful friction in the publication process for unverified claims, and technical partnerships with forensic experts who can provide authoritative assessment when needed. Tools help, but they are not the foundation. The foundation is editorial culture — a culture that treats the question "how do we know this is real?" as a professional obligation rather than an obstacle to publication.

Several organizations are building this kind of infrastructure. The BBC's Verify unit, established in 2017 and expanded significantly in 2023, operates on the principle that every significant claim in a story should be traceable to a primary source or direct evidence, and that synthetic media is just one category of claim that requires this treatment. The Associated Press has invested in a distributed network of regional fact-checking partnerships that can provide local context for media authenticity questions that generic detection tools cannot answer. The Pulitzer Prize-winning team at ProPublica has developed source verification protocols that treat digital media provenance as a routine part of investigative journalism, not a special investigation.

These are bright spots in a difficult picture. They do not scale easily, they are expensive to maintain, and they depend on editorial cultures that prioritize verification over speed — cultures that are increasingly rare in an industry under financial pressure. But they are proof that the problem is not unsolvable. The tools that newsrooms genuinely need — reliable provenance infrastructure, consistent verification protocols, and the institutional will to use them — are achievable. The question is whether the industry can build them fast enough to matter.

Conclusion: The Problem Is Not Technical

If there is a single conclusion that emerges from two years of research, investigation, and reporting on this topic, it is this: the newsroom deepfake crisis is not primarily a technical problem. The technical challenges are real and significant, but they are tractable. Better detection tools will be built. Better provenance standards will be adopted. Better compression-resistant forensic methods will be developed. These things will happen, because the market incentives and the research community are pointing in the same direction.

The harder problem is institutional. The news industry has built its production systems, its economic models, and its editorial cultures around the assumption that authentic information is available, distinguishable from fabricated information, and that the cost of verification is manageable. All three of those assumptions are now contested in ways they have not been before. Rebuilding institutions around different assumptions is slower, more expensive, and more culturally disruptive than building a better detection algorithm.

The journalists who are navigating this crisis today are doing so with inadequate tools, insufficient training, and institutional structures that were not designed for the threat they face. They are not failing because they are careless or because they do not understand the stakes. They are failing — when they fail — because the systems around them are not set up to support the decisions they are being asked to make. Fixing those systems is the work. It is not glamorous, and it will not produce a headline. But it is the only response that is actually adequate to the scale of the problem.
